Laika Studios

At the beginning

Laika’s history dates to 1976, when Oscar-winning animator Will Vinton opened Will Vinton Studios. The Portland studio had intermittent success, including the famous California Raisins “I Heard It Through the Grapevine” ad campaign.

The studio was struggling by 1998 when Nike co-founder Phil Knight made his first investment in it and his son, Travis Knight, joined the company as a junior animator. In 2003, Knight acquired the rest of the studio and forced Vinton out. In 2005, he renamed it Laika. Here’s a timeline of its film history.

2009

Laika releases its first film, "Coraline," directed by veteran Hollywood animator Henry Selick. It premieres in downtown Portland with searchlights and cast members including Teri Hatcher of "Desperate Housewives" fame. The film wins strong reviews and an Oscar nomination for best animated feature. It performed surprisingly well at the box office, too, grossing $125 million worldwide.

Laika scraps plans for a computer-animated feature and lays off 63. The studio chooses instead to focus on its signature stop-motion animation.



2012

Laika releases "ParaNorman," its second feature and first release aimed at the summer box-office crowd. The film's worldwide gross was $107 million, down from "Coraline," but it earned an Oscar nomination.

2014

“The Boxtrolls,” Laika’s third feature, opens. It, too, earns an Oscar nomination and grosses $109 million worldwide.

2016

Laika wins a technical award from the Oscars for 3-D printing technology that gives its characters more lifelike expression. The same year, “Kubo and the Two Strings” opens. It grosses $70 million and nets two Oscar nominations, for best animated feature and for visual effects.

2019

Laika's fifth film is "Missing Link". Hugh Jackman, Zoe Saldana, and Zach Galifianakis star in our most ambitious film to date - a rip-roaring, globetrotting comedy adventure.
